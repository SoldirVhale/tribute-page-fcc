J. Robert Oppenheimer:
The Architect of the Atomic Age

J. Robert Oppenheimer, often dubbed the "father of the atomic bomb," was a pivotal figure in 20th-century science and politics. As the scientific director of the Manhattan Project, Oppenheimer oversaw the development of the atomic bomb, a creation that would forever alter global diplomacy and warfare. His subsequent years were marked by his contributions to nuclear policy and his philosophical reflections on science and society.

    On July 16, 1945, the first atomic bomb was detonated at the Trinity site in New Mexico, marking a monumental achievement in science and engineering.
  • Post-War Years
    After the war, Oppenheimer became a chief advisor to the newly created United States Atomic Energy Commission, where he advocated for international control of nuclear power.
  • McCarthy Era Challenges
    During the 1950s, Oppenheimer faced significant political scrutiny over his earlier leftist associations and his stance on limiting nuclear arms, leading to a public hearing that stripped him of his security clearance.
